When coordinating a project, regardless of the methodology of implementation, a general hierarchy exists that clearly will define the roles of all the players involved. Agile is no different. Using Scrum as an example there exist a number of clearly defined roles which form the backbone of an Agile-based project. First we have the client who has realized an unfulfilled need exists which they do not have the means to manage. The client has a framework of ideas that have not fully been realized so they seek out a partnership with our Agile team.


The first point of contact in our Agile team is the Product Owner. This role is the liaison between our client and the Scrum Master and ultimately has the final say. Wilson, Brown, and Burke (2013). Their sole purpose is to collaborate extensively with the client to ensure that the client knows exactly what they want. Full authority to accept or reject proposals lies upon this role as the Product Owner when fulfilling their duties. This level of authority also extends to the Product Backlog, which is a culmination of all project requests broken down into phases known as sprints. Each sprint will have what is known as user stories which are really just features which the client and the Product Owner have agreed upon.

The third defined role is the Scrum Master (briefly mentioned previously). Whereas the Product Owner owns the product, the Scrum Master owns the process. It is the duty of the Scrum Master to ensure that the Agile development teams are able to work cohesively, free of distraction, and following clearly defined processes from the Agile model. To avoid becoming a distraction themselves, a Scrum Master also limits the amount of time spent interacting with the team throughout the day, but performing a Daily Scrum Meeting - a 15-minute meeting with developers to discuss completed user stories, future user stories, and impedances. The Daily Scrum meeting also is used to close out an existing sprint or kick-off a new one.

The Client, Product Owner, and Scrum Master are the most clearly defined roles in an Agile Development Model but other teams can, and do exist. The members of the Agile team which the Scrum Master assists are generally referred to as cross-functional group which blends a variety of roles; this is in contrast to a functional group which may be comprised solely of developers or testers. During a sprint the user stories would then be assigned to each group appropriately based upon the existing skill set and experience of the members. In some cases, a highly skilled individual may be shared between two groups but as this is viewed as a distraction and has potential to disrupt the process it is generally frowned upon.
